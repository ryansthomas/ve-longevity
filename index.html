The same discipline that builds better infrastructure can be applied to the most important project you'll ever manage — your own healthspan.

Civil & Transportation Engineering ◆ Longevity Science ◆ Thought Leadership

Value Engineering doesn't ask "what can we add?" It asks "what is this actually supposed to do — and what's the most effective way to achieve it?"

After decades in civil and transportation engineering, I've spent thousands of hours applying Value Engineering methodology to highways, bridges, and municipal infrastructure. The discipline is brutal in the best way: strip everything back to function, then rebuild only what delivers real value.

A few years ago, deep in the research on human longevity science — the work coming out of labs studying VO₂ max, insulin sensitivity, muscle mass preservation, and the hallmarks of cellular aging — I had an uncomfortable realization: we are terrible Value Engineers of our own bodies.

We add. We accumulate. We defer maintenance. We over-specify comfort and under-specify performance. And we rarely ask the most important VE question of all: What is the intended function of a long life, and what is the minimum effective dose to achieve it?

The VE Job Plan, Applied to You

Formal Value Engineering follows a structured Job Plan — Information, Function Analysis, Creative, Evaluation, Development, Presentation. Run your health through that same lens:

  • Information Phase — Gather the Data VE starts with hard data, not assumptions. For healthspan, this means knowing your actual numbers: VO₂ max (the single strongest predictor of all-cause mortality), fasting glucose and insulin, DEXA body composition, grip strength, resting heart rate variability. Most people are managing a project with no drawings.
  • Function Analysis — Define What "Working" Means A bridge's function is to transfer load safely and economically. What's yours? For most of us it isn't just avoiding death — it's preserving cognitive sharpness, physical independence, and emotional presence into our 80s and 90s. Clarifying function changes everything. It shifts the target from lifespan to healthspan.
  • Creative Phase — Challenge Every Assumption VE demands creative alternatives without judgment. What if the thing you've always done — the long slow cardio, the avoidance of red meat, the focus on daily stretching over strength — isn't the highest-value path? The longevity literature is full of counterintuitive findings. Zone 2 cardio and heavy resistance training together outperform either alone. Protein requirements for muscle preservation are far higher than traditional guidance suggests.
  • Evaluation — Rank by Function-to-Cost Ratio Not all interventions are equal. In VE, we pursue changes that deliver the most function per dollar spent. In longevity science, the highest-ROI interventions are strikingly consistent: VO₂ max training, resistance exercise, sleep quality, and metabolic health. Everything else — the supplements, the biohacks, the cold plunges — sits further down the value curve.
  • Development — Build the Alternative VE proposals require a complete alternative, not just a critique. This is where longevity science gets practical: structured exercise prescriptions, dietary protein targets, sleep architecture optimization, stress regulation protocols. Specificity is what separates a VE proposal from wishful thinking.
  • Presentation — Communicate & Commit Every VE study ends with a proposal that requires a decision. Your health is no different. The research means nothing without commitment to implementation. The best longevity protocol is the one you actually execute — consistently, over decades.

The Uncomfortable Truth About Deferred Maintenance

In infrastructure, deferred maintenance is the enemy of value. A pothole costs $50 to fill and $5,000 to repave around. Every VE practitioner knows the compounding cost of avoidance.

Human physiology works identically. VO₂ max declines approximately 10% per decade after age 30 without targeted intervention. Muscle mass loss — sarcopenia — begins in your 30s and accelerates sharply past 60. These are not surprises that arrive at retirement. They are slow-motion infrastructure failures in progress right now, in people who are deferring the maintenance.

The longevity researchers I follow — Peter Attia, David Sinclair, Rhonda Patrick — aren't describing exotic interventions. They're describing systematic, disciplined maintenance programs. The same logic that drives a pavement management system applies: intervene early, intervene specifically, and measure the outcomes.

The Bottom Line

Value Engineering taught me to ask hard questions about what something is actually worth. Applied to health, the answer is clarifying: the highest-value investment most of us can make isn't in our portfolio, our home, or our career — it's in the physical and cognitive capacity to enjoy all of it for decades longer than the average trajectory suggests we will. That's not a wellness platitude. That's a function analysis.

The principles that built our infrastructure can build a longer, stronger life — if we have the discipline to apply them with the same rigor. In both domains, the work is never glamorous. It's a study, a process, a proposal, and a commitment to execution. But the ROI is generational.
